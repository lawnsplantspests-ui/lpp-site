Why Is My Cherry Tree Sending Up Shoots Below the Graft?

Those vigorous shoots coming up from the base of your flowering cherry aren't a bonus — they're rootstock suckers, and they'll outcompete your tree if you ignore them.

What's Actually Happening

Most flowering cherries, fruit trees, and ornamental cherries sold at nurseries are grafted. A vigorous, hardy rootstock is grown from seed or cutting, and the desired variety (the cultivar you actually paid for — say, a Kwanzan or Yoshino cherry) is grafted onto it. You can usually see the graft union as a slight bulge or angled scar 4–12 inches above the soil line.

Everything above the graft is the variety you want. Everything below the graft is the rootstock — which is selected for vigor and disease resistance, not for showy flowers or fruit. When the rootstock sends up shoots (called "suckers" or "watershoots"), those shoots are rootstock genetics. They will not produce the flowers or fruit you bought the tree for.

Why Are They Appearing Now?

Rootstock suckers show up most often when the grafted top is stressed: drought, transplant shock, mechanical damage to the trunk, severe pruning, or disease. The rootstock senses reduced photosynthetic output from the top and pushes up its own shoots to "help."

In Central PA we see suckers commonly on:

  • Flowering cherries (especially after drought stretches)
  • Apple trees and crabapples
  • Peach and plum trees
  • Ornamental pears
  • Grafted roses
  • Lilacs (grafted onto privet rootstock — common at older landscapes)

Why You Can't Just Leave Them

Rootstock genetics are typically more vigorous than the grafted variety. If left alone, suckers will:

  • Steal energy from the desired top growth — leading to fewer flowers and weaker overall structure
  • Eventually overtake the graft entirely, especially if the top dies back during a hard winter or drought
  • Spread horizontally via underground runners on some species (especially in the Prunus family), popping up several feet from the trunk in your lawn beds

How to Remove Them Correctly

Don't Just Cut at Ground Level

Cutting suckers off at the soil surface with pruners is the most common mistake. The remaining buds and rootstock below will sprout three more shoots for every one you remove. This is the same response trees have to pruning above ground — you're triggering more vigorous regrowth.

Excavate Down to the Source

Gently scrape soil away to expose the base of each sucker. You'll see that the sucker emerges from a root or from the rootstock crown. Cut it as close to that origin point as possible — not at ground level. This removes the dormant buds that would otherwise produce replacement shoots.

Tear, Don't Cut, When Possible

On young suckers (less than pencil-thick), gripping at the base and tearing the sucker off downward — like pulling weeds — removes more of the bud tissue than a clean cut. The wound heals fine, and regrowth is much slower.

Timing Matters

Late spring through early summer is ideal. Cool, moist soil makes excavation easier and the tree heals quickly. Avoid removing suckers in fall — wounds heal slowly and provide entry points for winter pathogens.

Avoid Herbicides on Suckers Glyphosate or other systemic herbicides applied to rootstock suckers will travel through the connected root system into the grafted top. We've seen homeowners kill expensive ornamental cherries this way. There's no shortcut here — mechanical removal is the only safe method.

Underlying Causes to Address

Repeated heavy sucker production usually means the tree is stressed. Worth investigating:

  • Drought stress — water deeply (1 inch per week) during dry summer stretches, especially the first 2–3 years after planting
  • Mulch piled against the trunk ("mulch volcanoes") — keep mulch 2–3 inches deep and pulled back from the trunk by 4–6 inches
  • Mower or trimmer damage at the base — install a 3-foot mulch ring to keep equipment away
  • Soil compaction from foot traffic or construction — core aeration around the root zone can help
  • Disease in the top growth — black knot, cherry leaf spot, and fire blight all stress flowering cherries
