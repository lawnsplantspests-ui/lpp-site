Why Fall, Not Spring

Cool-season grasses — tall fescue, Kentucky bluegrass, perennial ryegrass — germinate best when soil is 55–70°F and the air is mild. In Central PA that happens twice a year: late March/April and mid-August through October. But spring seeding fails for three reasons:

  • Crabgrass season. Crabgrass germinates at the same temperatures as desirable grass. New grass seedlings can't outcompete crabgrass for water and sunlight.
  • Summer heat is coming. Young seedlings barely establish before midsummer drought stress kills them.
  • You can't use pre-emergent. Pre-emergent crabgrass control kills any seed it touches — including grass seed. Spring overseeding means skipping pre-emergent entirely and getting more crabgrass.

Fall is the opposite — crabgrass is dying off, the heat is dropping, weed pressure is low, and seedlings have 6–8 weeks of cool growing weather before winter dormancy.

The Central PA Fall Window

The biological seeding window for Harrisburg-area cool-season grasses runs August 15 through September 20. But we schedule our own core aeration and overseeding work only during the first weeks of September — that's the consistent sweet spot for soil temperature, moisture, and root establishment year after year. Booking early in the window also gives seedlings 6+ weeks of mild fall weather before winter dormancy.

Outside that window:

  • Before August 15: soil is still too hot in many years; seed will germinate unevenly and seedlings will struggle.
  • September 20–October 15: still workable but riskier — seedlings may not establish enough root before the first hard frost.
  • After mid-October: too late for new establishment. "Dormant seeding" in late November works for some grasses but is more of a Hail Mary than a plan.
When we book: aeration appointments open in July and fill by Labor Day every year. Call early if you want a slot in the September window.

What Overseeding Looks Like, Done Right

Just throwing seed on top of an existing lawn rarely works — grass seed needs to contact bare soil to germinate. The full process for a renovation looks like this:

1. Core Aeration

Pulled cores break up surface compaction, expose soil, and give seed somewhere to land that actually has moisture and root space. We don't recommend "spike" aeration — it compacts the soil more, not less. Pulled-core machines only.

2. Overseed With the Right Mix

For most Central PA lawns, a turf-type tall fescue blend is the workhorse — drought tolerant, disease resistant, holds up to foot traffic, stays green into late fall. Add some Kentucky bluegrass for fill-in and self-repair. Skip the cheap "contractor mix" with annual ryegrass; it's a one-year band-aid.

3. Starter Fertilizer

A light starter fertilizer with phosphorus (which Pennsylvania regulates — only legal at seeding or when soil tests low) feeds the seedlings during root establishment. Apply at seeding, then again at 4 weeks.

4. Water Right

The first 2 weeks: light watering 2–3 times a day to keep the surface moist (don't let it dry out — seedlings die in hours, not days). Weeks 3–4: cut back to once a day, deeper. After establishment: normal 1-inch-per-week deep watering. Cool fall weather makes this easier than spring or summer.

5. Don't Mow Too Soon, Or Too Short

Let new grass reach 4 inches before first mow. Cut to 3 inches, no shorter. Mow with a sharp blade — a dull blade rips young seedlings out of the soil instead of cutting them.

What About Herbicides After Seeding?

Most broadleaf herbicides will damage or kill seedlings under 4–6 weeks old. If you're overseeding, plan to skip the fall broadleaf application or apply it at least 4 weeks before seeding. We sequence the fall calendar for clients so the weed control and overseeding don't conflict.
