Why Is My Grass Turning Brown in July?

Cool-season lawns in Central PA take a beating in midsummer. Drought, grubs, and fungal disease all cause brown patches — but each one needs a different response.

The Three Most Common Causes

If your lawn was green in June and brown in July, it's almost always one of three things. Diagnosing correctly matters because the treatment for one can make another worse.

1. Drought Stress (Summer Dormancy)

Tall fescue and Kentucky bluegrass — the two most common lawn grasses in the Harrisburg area — naturally go dormant when soil temperatures climb above 80°F and rainfall drops. The lawn turns straw-colored, the blades feel crisp, and growth stops. This is normal. The crowns stay alive even when the leaves brown out.

How to spot it: Brown is uniform across the lawn, worst on south-facing slopes and near pavement. Tug a handful of grass — it stays rooted. No insect damage visible at the soil line.

What to do: Either accept the dormancy (the lawn will green up after the next stretch of cooler, wetter weather), or water deeply once a week. 1 inch of water, applied early morning, soaks down 6 inches and keeps the crowns active. Light daily watering causes more harm than good.

2. White Grub Damage

Grubs — the larval form of Japanese beetles, June beetles, and masked chafers — feed on grass roots starting in midsummer. By late July you'll see irregular brown patches that look like drought but don't recover with watering.

How to spot it: Brown patches are irregular, not uniform. Tug a handful of grass — it pulls up like a piece of carpet because the roots are gone. Dig a 1-square-foot section 2–3 inches deep at the edge of a brown spot. If you find 6+ C-shaped white grubs, you have an infestation.

What to do: If it's already late July or August, curative grub control is the only option — and recovery this season is unlikely. The right move for next year is a preventive treatment applied in late June, before grubs hatch. Watering brown spots doesn't help once roots are chewed off.

3. Summer Fungal Disease

Brown patch (Rhizoctonia) and dollar spot are the two most common fungal diseases on Central PA lawns in summer. Both peak during humid stretches in the 75–90°F range — exactly our July weather.

How to spot it: Brown patch shows up as circular or irregular brown areas, often with a faint "smoke ring" at the edge in early morning. Dollar spot shows small (silver dollar-sized) bleached patches that merge into larger areas. Both are worst in lawns that got watered in the evening or fertilized heavily in spring.

What to do: Stop watering in the evening — water early morning only so the lawn dries fast. Mow higher (3.5–4 inches) to reduce stress. Fungicide treatments work but are usually only worth it on high-value lawns; for most properties, cultural changes (mowing height, watering timing, reduced nitrogen) prevent recurrence.

Quick Diagnostic Test Pull a handful of grass at the edge of a brown spot. If it resists — roots intact — it's drought or fungus. If it lifts off like a rug — roots are gone — it's grubs.

What NOT to Do in July

  • Don't fertilize. Synthetic nitrogen in heat stresses dormant grass and can burn it permanently. Save the feeding for September.
  • Don't apply broadleaf herbicide. Most weed killers damage cool-season turf at high temperatures. Wait for cooler weather.
  • Don't core aerate or overseed. The window for that is late August through mid-September in Central PA — not before.
  • Don't water lightly every day. Shallow watering creates shallow roots. One deep inch per week, early morning, is right.
