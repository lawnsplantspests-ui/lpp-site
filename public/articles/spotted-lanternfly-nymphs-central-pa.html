The Four Nymph Stages

Spotted lanternfly (Lycorma delicatula) goes through four nymph stages — called instars — before becoming the spotted adult most people recognize. In Central PA the eggs hatch in early-to-mid May, and the nymphs cycle through the four stages from May into July. If you're treating, this is the season that matters.

Instars 1–3: Black with White Spots

The first three nymph stages all look similar — small (¼ to ½ inch), wingless, jet black with bright white spots. They're easy to mistake for ticks at a glance, but they move fast and jump when disturbed. You'll find them clustered on tree of heaven (Ailanthus altissima), grapevines, maples, walnuts, and willows.

Instar 4: Red with Black and White Spots

The fourth nymph stage shows up in late June through July and looks dramatically different — bright red with black stripes and white spots. Same body size, same behavior, same host trees, but unmistakably red. This is the stage many homeowners notice first, because the color stands out.

Quick rule: Black with white spots = early summer (May–June). Red with white spots = mid-to-late July. Spotted adults with grey wings = August into November.

What They Damage

Spotted lanternfly feeds by piercing bark and stems and sucking sap. The damage shows up three ways:

  • Stress on host trees — heavy populations weaken maples, walnuts, and tree of heaven. Repeated years of feeding can kill stressed trees outright.
  • Honeydew and sooty mold — the bugs excrete sticky honeydew that drips onto everything below. Cars, patios, decks, and understory plants get coated in black sooty mold that grows on the honeydew.
  • Yellow jackets and wasps — the honeydew attracts wasps in huge numbers. If you have a backyard infestation, expect to be sharing the deck with stinging insects all summer.

Grapevines, hops, and fruit trees take the worst hit — lanternfly can kill grapevines outright in a single season. For the average Harrisburg homeowner, the biggest practical problem is the honeydew, the wasps, and the long-term damage to mature shade trees.

When Treatment Actually Works

Two windows matter most in Central PA:

1. May through mid-July: Nymph Stage

Nymphs are small, clustered, and easier to kill than adults. Treatment during this window protects host trees before damage accumulates. Soil-applied systemic insecticides take 4–6 weeks to move through the tree, so applications made in April or early May are working by the time nymphs are feeding.

2. Late August through October: Adult Stage

Adults are larger, more mobile, and harder to kill — but this is the stage that lays eggs. Treatments here are about reducing next year's population. Bark contact treatments and trunk sprays work well for ornamental trees.

The Pollinator Problem

Many spotted lanternfly treatments — especially imidacloprid soil drenches and dinotefuran trunk sprays — are highly toxic to bees if applied to a tree that's flowering or about to flower. Don't treat a flowering tree. Wait until bloom is over.

What You Can Do Yourself

  • Remove tree of heaven from your property. Tree of heaven is the lanternfly's preferred host. Even one or two removed trees on your property dramatically reduces local pressure.
  • Stomp them. Pennsylvania's Department of Agriculture explicitly asks residents to kill lanternfly on sight. It won't eliminate a population, but it helps.
  • Inspect anything outdoors for egg masses in fall and winter — they look like grey putty or smeared mud, often on tree bark, lawn furniture, firewood, and grills.
  • Don't put up sticky bands. They catch birds and beneficial insects. Use circle traps instead if you want to trap mechanically.
