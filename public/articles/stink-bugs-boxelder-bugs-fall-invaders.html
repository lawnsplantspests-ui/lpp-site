The "Overwintering" Group

Several Central PA insects share the same fall survival strategy: find a warm sheltered spot before the first hard frost, slow their metabolism way down, wait out the winter, and emerge in spring. Modern houses with south-facing siding and gaps around windows make perfect overwintering sites. The most common ones in Harrisburg-area homes:

  • Brown marmorated stink bug — invasive shield-shaped bug, the original fall-invader complaint in PA
  • Boxelder bug — black with red markings, congregates on sunny walls in October
  • Asian (multicolored) lady beetle — looks like a ladybug, bites when handled, smells when crushed
  • Western conifer seed bug — long brown bug, often mistaken for a stink bug
  • Cluster fly — slightly larger than houseflies, sluggish, shows up in attic spaces and upstairs windows

They don't bite, don't breed inside, don't damage anything structurally — but they're a real nuisance, and once they're in the wall void, getting them out is a months-long problem.

Why They Pick Your House

Three things make a house attractive:

  1. South or southwest exposure. Warm afternoon sun in late September signals "good winter spot."
  2. Gaps and seams. Soffits, window frames, vent screens, around plumbing penetrations, where siding meets trim. Anything bigger than 1/8 inch is a doorway.
  3. Surrounding trees and crops. Stink bugs come from nearby trees and gardens. Boxelder bugs come from — surprise — boxelder trees and maples. Asian lady beetles come from soybean fields and woodlots. Properties bordering woods or farms see the worst pressure.
Timing is everything: the only effective treatment window is before they try to enter. Once they're in the wall voids, sprays don't reach them. The right window in Central PA is late September through mid-October, before the first cold snap drives them in.

What Actually Works

1. Exterior Perimeter Treatment

A residual insecticide applied to the exterior surfaces where these bugs land — siding, around windows, door frames, soffits, gable ends, vents — kills them on contact and for 4–6 weeks after. Done once in late September or early October, this is the single most effective step. Done after they're already inside, it's useless.

2. Seal the Gaps

Caulk gaps around window frames, door frames, plumbing and electrical penetrations, where siding meets trim. Replace torn or missing screens, including soffit and gable vent screens. This is the slow, permanent fix and worth doing even if you're also having the perimeter treated.

3. Remove the Source Trees (If Realistic)

Boxelder bugs specifically come from female boxelder trees. If you have one within a few hundred feet of the house, you'll fight boxelder bugs forever. Stink bugs and Asian lady beetles come from broader sources you can't realistically remove.

What Doesn't Work

  • Bug bombs and foggers inside the house. They don't reach insects already deep in wall voids and they leave heavy chemical residue on living surfaces. Skip them.
  • Spraying inside walls after they're in. By December the bugs are dormant inside insulation and behind drywall. No interior treatment reaches them.
  • One-shot consumer sprays applied in November. Too late and short residual.

What to Do If They're Already Inside

If you're seeing stink bugs or boxelder bugs in the house in November, the colony is already in your walls. Don't crush them (the smell attracts more) — vacuum them with a shop vac, empty the bag/canister outside, and accept that you'll keep finding stragglers through spring. Then schedule exterior treatment for the following September so it doesn't happen again.
