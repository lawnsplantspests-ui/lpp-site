The Annual Yellow Jacket Cycle

Yellow jackets in Central PA build a brand new nest every year — they don't reuse old ones. Understanding the timeline explains why a property that had no problem in May suddenly has 50 wasps on the picnic table in August.

April–May: One Queen, Tiny Nest

A single fertilized queen wakes up from overwintering and starts a new nest from scratch. She lays the first eggs, raises the first workers, and the nest is the size of a golf ball. You won't notice it.

June–July: 200–500 Workers

Workers take over feeding the colony. The queen does nothing but lay eggs. The nest doubles in size every couple of weeks. Most homeowners notice activity now — a steady stream of wasps coming and going from a hole in the lawn, a soffit, a porch joist, or a bush.

August–September: 2,000–5,000 Workers (Peak)

This is the dangerous window. The colony is at maximum size right when natural food sources (caterpillars, soft fruit, garbage) start dropping off. The workers get desperate, switch from hunting to scavenging, and start swarming everything sweet or meaty on your deck. They sting on much less provocation than they did in June.

October–November: Collapse

The first hard frost kills the colony. Only new queens (mated daughters) survive, find a sheltered spot to overwinter, and start the cycle next spring on a different patch of your property.

Why August stings hurt more: they don't. Late-summer stings happen more because there are 10× more workers per nest, they're hungrier, and they're far more defensive. A nest you ignored in June will be a much bigger problem in September.

Where Yellow Jackets Nest in Central PA

Yellow jackets nest in two general categories — ground nests and aerial nests. Both common in the Harrisburg area:

  • Ground nests: abandoned rodent burrows, voids under shrubs, hollow stumps, retaining walls, mulch beds. Often invisible from above except for a steady stream of traffic going in and out of a hole the size of a quarter.
  • Aerial nests: soffits, attic vents, gable vents, porch ceilings, shed eaves, hollow wall voids. The classic "I hear buzzing in the wall" call.

Note: a basketball-sized grey papery nest hanging visibly from a tree branch is almost always a bald-faced hornet (not yellow jackets). Same family, similar behavior, and we remove them too — but the nest shape is the giveaway.

What Not to Do

  • Don't pour gasoline down a ground nest. It's illegal, it's a fire hazard, it contaminates groundwater, and it doesn't reliably kill the queen.
  • Don't seal the entrance to an aerial nest in the wall. The colony will chew through drywall or insulation to escape — and end up inside your house.
  • Don't approach a nest at midday. Workers are most active and most defensive between 10 AM and 6 PM. Yellow jackets can sting repeatedly and will pursue you 30+ feet.
  • Don't use big-box hornet spray on a soffit nest you can't see. Spraying at the entrance just kills the foragers — the queen and the brood are 2 feet inside the wall.

How We Handle Active Nests

Ground nests get treated late evening or after dark, when workers are inside. We use a low-volume insecticide dust pushed deep into the entrance — kills the foragers as they pass through, then carries into the nest and kills the queen and brood. Usually one treatment.

Aerial nests in soffits and walls are similar — dust applied to the entrance with extension tools, then the entrance is left open for 48–72 hours so returning workers contact the residue. We don't seal the hole until the nest is confirmed dead, for the reason above.

Visible aerial nests (paper wasp or bald-faced hornet, hanging from a branch or under a porch ceiling) are knock-down jobs with the right gear and PPE.
