The Four Flies That Cost You Money

Horn Flies

The little dark flies riding backs and sides by the hundreds, taking 20+ blood meals a day each. The rule-of-thumb economic threshold is about 200 per animal — past that, they're eating measurable weight gain and milk. Pasture cattle wear them all season if nothing intervenes.

Face Flies

The ones clustered on eyes and muzzles, feeding on secretions — and spreading pinkeye through the herd while they're at it. Controlling face flies is half the battle in a pinkeye-prone group.

Stable Flies

Fierce leg-biters that make cattle stomp, bunch, and stand in water instead of eating. They breed in wet mixed organic matter — spilled feed, old bale rings, manure-straw pack — which is why sanitation is part of any honest program.

House Flies

The barn and parlor nuisance: disease-vectoring, residue-leaving, neighbor-annoying. Numbers explode wherever manure and moisture sit undisturbed for more than a week.

What a Whole-Farm Fly Program Looks Like

One method never holds for a season — the operations that stay ahead of flies layer several and rotate their chemistry so resistance doesn't quietly cancel the program.

On-Animal Control

Pour-ons, sprays, and dust — timed to fly pressure, matched to your handling reality (dairy stanchions vs. beef on pasture), and always with milk and slaughter label rules front and center.

Self-Treatment Setups

Back rubbers and dust bags at gateways and mineral stations put control on the cattle's schedule, not yours. We site them, charge them, and keep them working through the season.

Premise Treatment

Residual applications on barn walls, parlors, and fly resting surfaces, plus baits where they earn their keep. The barn is treated as its own battlefield.

Breeding-Site Strategy

Every fly on a cow started in wet organic matter somewhere on the property. Manure handling rhythm, spilled-feed cleanup, and drainage fixes cut the population at the source — the least glamorous work and the highest-percentage play.

Resistance Rotation

Horn flies develop pyrethroid resistance fast when the same chemistry runs for years. We track what's been used and rotate mode of action deliberately — the difference between a program and a habit.

Cattle Fly FAQ

When should fly control start in Central PA?
Before you're sick of them — fly populations build exponentially, so knocking down the early generations in late spring pays all season. Starting in August means fighting the whole summer's compound interest.
Is treatment safe for lactating dairy cows?
Products differ enormously — plenty are labeled for lactating dairy with zero milk withhold, others are beef-only. Reading and following those labels is literally the licensed part of licensed application; you'll know exactly what's used and what it's approved for.
We used the same pour-on for years and it stopped working. Resistance?
Very likely, especially with horn flies and pyrethroids — it's one of the best-documented resistance stories in agriculture. The fix is rotating to a different mode of action on a plan, not doubling the dose of the one that quit.
Do fly predators (parasitic wasps) work?
They can help suppress house and stable flies around confined areas as part of a program — they're not a stand-alone fix, and they do nothing for horn flies on pasture. If you're using them, we'll build treatments around them; broadcast spraying their release areas wastes both investments.
