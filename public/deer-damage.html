Why Deer Damage Is Getting Worse in Central PA

Suburban deer populations in Dauphin, Cumberland, Lebanon, Perry, and York counties keep climbing. With fewer hunters and more cul-de-sac neighborhoods backing to woods, herds get bigger and more habituated to landscaped yards. By late winter when natural browse runs out, deer hit arborvitae, yews, hostas, hydrangea, and rhododendron hard — and once a plant gets stripped to bare wood, recovery can take years.

We treat preventatively before damage starts and run protection programs that keep deer pressure off the plants you've invested in.

Our Deer Damage Services

Repellent Applications

Rotating products and spray timing so deer don't habituate. We apply commercial-grade repellents that hold up through rain better than off-the-shelf homeowner products — applied with proper coverage on undersides of leaves and growing tips where deer browse first.

Winter Browse Protection

The worst damage happens December through March on arborvitae, yews, and other evergreens. We set up a seasonal program with timed repellent applications through the cold months when deer pressure peaks.

Spring & Summer Hosta Protection

Hostas, daylilies, and tulips are top deer targets in spring. We start treatments early — before the first deer hits — and re-apply through the season so plants stay intact for the visible months you actually use the yard.

Fruit Tree & Ornamental Protection

Young fruit trees, ornamental cherries, dogwoods, and rose of sharon are vulnerable to both browse damage and antler rub. We treat foliage and recommend trunk protection where rub damage is the bigger risk.

Damage Assessment & Recovery Guidance

For plants already chewed up — we walk your property, identify which can recover and which should be replaced, and recommend pruning timing and follow-up care. Arborvitae stripped below the green is usually a replacement decision; lightly browsed shrubs usually bounce back.

Browse-Resistant Plant Recommendations

If deer keep hitting the same spot, sometimes the right answer is a different plant. We can recommend deer-resistant alternatives for your specific conditions — Central PA shade, sun, clay soils, slopes — without pretending any plant is truly deer-proof.

Most-Damaged Plants in Central PA

  • Arborvitae (Thuja occidentalis) — the #1 deer target in suburban Central PA. Devastated December through March.
  • Yews (Taxus) — second most-browsed. Plants can be stripped quickly during hard winters.
  • Hostas — heavily browsed spring and summer. Often eaten to the crown overnight.
  • Daylilies, tulips, and most spring bulbs — exception: daffodils, which deer avoid.
  • Rhododendron, azalea, hydrangea — winter browse and bud damage.
  • Young fruit trees — both browse and antler-rub damage.

Frequently Asked Questions

How often do repellents need to be applied?
Every 4–6 weeks during active deer pressure, more often after heavy rain or new growth. Winter browse season on arborvitae and yews needs the most consistent schedule — once a plant gets a few deer-free weeks, browsing can spike when the repellent fades.
Are repellents safe for kids, pets, and birds?
Yes when applied as directed. We use commercial-grade products that are EPA-registered and applied by a licensed Pennsylvania pesticide applicator. Most modern deer repellents are essential-oil-based or putrescent-egg-based — not systemic insecticides.
Can I just install deer fencing instead?
For large gardens or full yard protection, deer fencing is the most reliable solution but requires 7'+ height to actually exclude deer. For most properties, repellent programs are easier, cheaper, and visually less intrusive — and they protect specific high-value plants without fencing the whole yard.
Will my stripped arborvitae grow back?
It depends on how far down the deer went. Arborvitae only regrows from green needles — if a section is chewed to bare brown wood, that section won't come back. Light browse on the outer foliage usually recovers in a season or two with good care.
What plants do deer actually leave alone?
Truly deer-resistant options in Central PA include boxwood, ornamental grasses, lavender, Russian sage, daffodils, bleeding heart, ferns, peonies, and most herbs. "Deer-resistant" doesn't mean deer-proof — in a hard winter even these can get sampled.
