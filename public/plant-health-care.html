Why Diagnosis-First Plant Health Care Matters

Most plant problems look similar from a distance — yellowing needles, browning leaves, dropped foliage — but the right treatment depends entirely on the underlying cause. Needle cast disease and drought stress can look the same. Aphids and spider mites cause similar damage but require different products. We diagnose before we treat, so the right product goes on the right plant at the right time.

Root Zone Fertilization

Injects slow-release fertilizer directly into the root zone. Especially valuable for older trees, recently planted trees, and any tree showing thin canopy or poor leaf color.

Foliar Fertilization

Liquid nutrient application directly to the leaf surface. Fast uptake for trees and shrubs showing acute nutrient deficiency, transplant shock, or summer stress. Useful between regular feedings when a plant needs a quick boost.

Dormant Oil Applications

Applied in late winter while plants are dormant, dormant oil smothers overwintering insect eggs — scale insects, mites, aphids. One of the most effective and lowest-impact treatments available.

Organic Plant Health Care (Neem Oil-Based)

Neem oil acts as an antifeedant, repellent, and fungicide against many soft-bodied insects (aphids, mites, whiteflies, scale crawlers) and against powdery mildew and rust. Common Tree & Shrub Problems in Central PA

  • Spotted lanternfly — on tree of heaven, maples, walnuts, grape vines. Worst late summer.
  • Emerald ash borer — has killed most untreated ash trees in PA. Surviving ash need ongoing soil-applied protection.
  • Bagworms — on arborvitae, junipers, spruces. Treatment window is May to mid-June while caterpillars are small.
  • Hemlock woolly adelgid — on hemlocks throughout Central PA. Without treatment, hemlocks decline and die over 4–10 years.
  • Fire blight — on apples, pears, crabapples, serviceberries. Causes dramatic browning that looks like a torch ran through the branches.
  • Spruce decline (Rhizosphaera, Stigmina) — needle cast diseases on Colorado blue spruce. Treatable but requires multi-year programs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will your treatments harm bees and pollinators?
We work hard to make sure they don't. We use soil drenches instead of foliar sprays where possible, time applications before or after bloom on flowering plants, and choose products that target the specific pest rather than broad-spectrum kill-everything products. We're a working beekeeper — protecting pollinators isn't a marketing line, it's how we run our business.
Do you do tree pruning or tree removal?
No. We focus on treatments — insect control, disease management, fertilization, soil work, and lanternfly. For pruning, deadwood removal, or tree removal you'll need a certified arborist.
Soil drenches vs. trunk injections — what's the difference?
Trunk injections deliver product through holes drilled into the trunk — each injection wound is a permanent injury. Soil drenches apply product to the root zone, where roots take it up and distribute it through the tree. For most chronic insect issues we recommend soil drenches.
When should I worry about spotted lanternfly?
If you have tree of heaven, maples, walnuts, willows, or grape vines, you'll start seeing nymphs in May and adults from July through fall. Heavy infestations stress trees and drip honeydew that attracts wasps and mold. The most effective control is preventative — treat host trees in spring.
