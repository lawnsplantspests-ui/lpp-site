Which Algae Do You Have? It Changes the Treatment

"Algae" is three different problems wearing one name, and the right product and timing depend on which one is in your pond. Treating the wrong one wastes your money — we see it constantly on self-treated ponds.

Filamentous Algae ("Pond Scum")

The stringy, cottony mats that start on the bottom in spring, float up as they produce oxygen, and blanket the surface by June. The most common algae call we get in Harrisburg — and the most treatable, if it's staged correctly.

Planktonic Algae ("Green Water")

Microscopic algae that turn the whole pond pea-soup green. Some bloom is normal and even feeds the food chain — runaway blooms mean a nutrient problem, and a sudden die-off can crash your oxygen and kill fish overnight.

Chara & Bottom Growth

Feels crunchy, smells musky, looks like a plant but it's algae. Chara carpets pond bottoms and laughs at products meant for surface scum. Different treatment entirely.

Harmful Algal Blooms

Blue-green "algae" (cyanobacteria) can produce toxins dangerous to dogs, livestock, and people. Paint-like blue-green surface streaks deserve caution — keep pets out of the water and have it looked at before anyone swims.

How We Treat Algae Without Hurting Your Pond

Anyone can dump copper in a pond. Doing it well looks different:

Identify First

Species and coverage determine product, rate, and timing. Water temperature matters too — treat too early in cold water and you've wasted a treatment.

Stage the Treatment

The biggest fish-kill risk isn't the product — it's decomposition. Kill an entire pond's algae in one hot week and the decay strips the oxygen out of the water. Heavily covered ponds get treated in sections.

Fix the Cause

Algae is a symptom. The disease is nutrients: lawn fertilizer runoff, goose droppings, leaf litter, and decades of bottom muck. We pair treatments with beneficial-bacteria muck reduction and practical nutrient advice so each year needs less product, not more.

Stay Legal

Many effective algaecides are restricted-use, and Pennsylvania requires DEP permitting for many aquatic applications. We're licensed for aquatic work and handle permits when required — see our full aquatic service.

Pond Algae FAQ

When should algae be treated in Central PA?
Filamentous algae is easiest to control early — spring, when mats are small and water passes 60°F. By July a fully blanketed pond needs staged treatment. Prevention programs start even earlier, before growth takes off.
Will treatment kill my fish?
At label rates the products are safe for fish — the danger is oxygen loss from too much algae decomposing at once, which is why we stage heavy ponds. Copper products also have limits around trout and some pond situations; that's part of why identification and licensing matter.
Why does my pond get worse every year?
Nutrients accumulate: every leaf, grass clipping, goose dropping, and fertilizer rain event feeds next year's bloom, and dying algae itself becomes bottom muck that feeds the next round. Breaking that cycle takes muck reduction and nutrient management, not just a yearly kill.
Is some algae actually good?
Yes — a living pond isn't a swimming pool. Light planktonic bloom and modest coverage (10–20%) feed the ecosystem that supports your fish, frogs, and dragonflies. Our pond restoration approach is built on that balance.
