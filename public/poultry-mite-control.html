Know Your Enemy: the Three Usual Suspects

They're all tiny, they're all miserable for the birds, and they're not treated the same way — because they don't live in the same place.

Northern Fowl Mite

Lives its whole life on the bird, concentrated around the vent. Look for dirty, greasy-looking vent feathers with clumps of mite debris. The most common mite in PA flocks, active year-round, and hard on lay rates — heavy loads genuinely drain birds.

Red Roost Mite (Chicken Mite)

The night-shift vampire: hides in coop cracks, roost-bar joints, and nest-box seams by day and feeds on sleeping birds by night. Check with a flashlight after dark or wipe a white paper towel under the roost — red smears mean mites. Because it lives in the housing, treating only the birds does nothing.

Poultry Lice

Fast-moving straw-colored insects at the feather base, with white egg clusters glued near the vent and under wings. More irritation than anemia, but heavy loads stress birds, hurt lay, and spread through a flock quickly.

How We Treat a Flock

Identify First

On-bird check (vent area, feather base) plus a housing inspection. Which pest and where it lives sets the whole plan.

Treat Birds AND Coop

Red mites demand housing treatment — cracks, roosts, nest boxes; fowl mites and lice demand on-bird treatment. Most failed DIY jobs treated one and not the other.

Repeat on Schedule

No product kills eggs waiting to hatch. Follow-up applications 7–10 days apart catch the next generation before it lays — skip the repeat and you've rented relief, not bought control.

Respect the Eggs

Laying flocks restrict which products are legal and how they're used. As licensed applicators we follow label rules written for food-producing birds — worth knowing before anyone sprays your breakfast supply with something from the garage shelf.

Prevent Reinfestation

Wild birds are the delivery service for mites — screening coop gaps, managing sparrow nests, quarantining new birds, and keeping dust-bath areas dry all lower the odds of a rerun. Practical advice included with every job.

Poultry Mite FAQ

Can I eat the eggs during treatment?
Depends entirely on the product — some are approved for laying hens with zero egg withdrawal, others aren't legal around layers at all. That's exactly the judgment a licensed applicator brings; we'll tell you the answer for your specific treatment before we start.
I dusted the birds twice and the mites came back. Why?
Two usual reasons: red roost mites living in the coop (the birds get re-boarded every night no matter how well you dust them), or the follow-up window was missed and the next hatch restarted the cycle. Sometimes both.
Do mites from chickens bite people?
They can — red mites and northern fowl mites will opportunistically bite whoever collects the eggs, and a heavy coop infestation can send mites wandering into attached garages or homes. Treating the source flock and housing ends it.
How do I check my flock without catching every bird?
Night flashlight check of the roost bars and a white-paper-towel wipe under the roosts finds red mites; grabbing a few birds and parting the vent feathers finds fowl mites and lice. Ten minutes a month catches problems while they're small — and if you'd rather we do it, flock checks ride along with any farm visit.
