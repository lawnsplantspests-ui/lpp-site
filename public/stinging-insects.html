What We Treat

Yellow Jackets

The most aggressive and most common stinging-insect call we get from late summer through fall. They build nests in the ground, wall voids, soffits, and under attic insulation. By August a single colony can have 2,000–5,000 workers. We treat at the entry point, then return to seal the void if needed. Most nests are knocked down within 24–48 hours of treatment.

Key FactsActive nests peak August through October in Central PA. Yellow jackets sting repeatedly — unlike honey bees which sting once. Allergic reactions are most common with yellow jackets. DIY spray often pushes them deeper into wall voids and creates a worse problem.

Bald-Faced Hornets

Build the iconic football-shaped gray paper nest hanging from tree limbs, under eaves, in gutters, and on houses. Despite the name they're actually a type of yellow jacket — but larger, more aggressive, and their sting carries more venom. A typical mature nest holds 400–700 workers and can be the size of a basketball.

Paper Wasps

Build open-cell umbrella-shaped nests under deck rails, eaves, soffits, mailboxes, grills, swing sets, and porch lights. Smaller colonies than yellow jackets — usually 20–30 workers. Less aggressive overall, but they will defend the nest if disturbed.

European Hornets

Large (up to 1.5 inches), reddish-brown and yellow, active at night — often drawn to lit windows. Typically nest in hollow trees, attic spaces, and wall voids. Less aggressive than yellow jackets but their large size makes them alarming.

Cicada Killers

Large (1.5–2 inches), striking yellow-and-black solitary wasps that dig burrows in lawn areas. Despite their intimidating size they're not aggressive — males can't sting at all. We treat them when activity is heavy in a high-traffic area, but in many cases the right answer is "leave them alone." We'll tell you which case you're in honestly — no upselling treatment for an insect that doesn't need it.

Carpenter Bees

Look like bumble bees but with a shiny hairless abdomen. They drill perfectly round half-inch holes into deck rails, fascia boards, eaves, fence posts, and exposed beams. The problem isn't safety — it's structural damage. We apply insecticidal dust into each active hole, then plug the holes so future generations can't reuse them.

Honey Bees — We Don't Spray ThemIf your stinging insects turn out to be honey bees, we don't exterminate them. We coordinate live removal with our partner apiary. See our Honey Bee Removal page.

Why DIY Often Makes the Problem Worse

  • Store-bought spray on a wall void nest: Yellow jackets get angry, abandon that entry, and chew new exits into your living space. We've responded to homes where 20–50 yellow jackets came out a ceiling vent the next day.
  • Knocking down a nest without treating first: A bald-faced hornet nest knocked down with a broom releases hundreds of angry hornets. They'll defend the location for hours.
  • Hot water or fire on ground nests: Boiling water reaches a few inches before cooling off. Gasoline and fire are dangerous, illegal, and ineffective.

How much does stinging insect removal cost?
A paper wasp nest under a porch eave is straightforward. A yellow jacket colony deep in a wall void takes more time and equipment. We provide free estimates and quote up front before any work begins.
Will the nest come back if you don't remove it physically?
For paper wasp and bald-faced hornet nests, we typically knock down the nest after treatment so the structure isn't there to attract reuse next season. For ground yellow jacket nests, the colony dies and the nest decomposes naturally.
