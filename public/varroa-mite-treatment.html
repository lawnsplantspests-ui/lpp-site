Monitoring Visits

Alcohol-wash counts on a schedule — the accurate method, done right (300-bee sample from the brood nest, queen located first). You get the number per hive and what it means for the calendar. Curious about the method? Our apiary site explains exactly how an alcohol wash works.

Threshold-Based Decisions

No calendar spraying, no product-of-the-month. Counts get compared against seasonal action thresholds — tighter in late summer, when the count decides whether your winter bees are raised healthy — and treatment happens when the numbers say so.

Treatment Application

The right product for the temperature, brood state, and honey-super situation, applied per label, with the timing details (dosing, spacing, duration) handled correctly. Chemistry gets rotated between treatments so resistance doesn't build in your apiary.

Post-Treatment Verification

A follow-up count confirms the treatment actually worked — the step almost everyone skips, and the reason "I treated in September" colonies still die in January. A treatment without a recheck is a hope, not a plan.

Whole-Apiary Health Checks

While we're in the hives: small hive beetle pressure, wax moth, and brood-disease red flags (American and European foulbrood) get flagged early, when options are good.

Varroa Service FAQ

I only have two hives. Is this worth it for me?
Two hives are exactly who loses colonies to varroa — big operations have monitoring routines; two-hive beekeepers have good intentions and busy Augusts. A few visits a season costs far less than replacing dead-outs every spring.
When do mite counts matter most in PA?
August, by a mile. The bees raised in September and October are the ones that must survive to March, and they have to be raised by low-mite, low-virus nurse bees. Our apiary site covers the seasonal thresholds in detail.
Can't I just treat every fall without counting?
Blind calendar treatment fails both directions: it misses June spikes that wreck colonies before fall, and it applies product to hives that didn't need it — building resistance in the mites that survive. Counts cost minutes and make every treatment decision better.
Why does it matter that you're also a beekeeper?
Because varroa work is beekeeping, not just application: finding queens before a wash, reading brood state, judging what a colony can tolerate in a heat wave, knowing when supers complicate product choice. We winter our own bees on the same decisions we'll make in your yard.
